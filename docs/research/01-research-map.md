# Research Map

This document tracks what Alpha needs to learn before becoming a product.

## Core Assumptions

1. AI-generated software will make custom tools less scarce.
2. The next scarce layer will be trusted orchestration of human intent.
3. People will value systems that shape lived experience, not just answer questions.
4. Social aliveness is a stronger consumer wedge than productivity.
5. Games and entertainment provide the grammar for making AI orchestration desirable.
6. Memory and taste are more defensible than generic agent execution.
7. The first product must create a felt shift within one session or one week.

## Research Threads

### Human Desire

Questions:

- What do people actually want AI to do in their lives beyond productivity?
- Where do people feel coordination pain most strongly?
- What kinds of AI involvement feel magical, and what kinds feel invasive?
- What does "aliveness" mean in measurable product terms?

### Social Groups

Questions:

- Which groups are most willing to try playful coordination?
- Are friend groups, families, teams, or communities the best wedge?
- Who is the buyer or initiator inside a group?
- What makes a group ritual persist?

### Entertainment And Gaming

Questions:

- Which game mechanics translate well into real life?
- What makes ambient games durable?
- How much fiction/lore is fun before it becomes embarrassing?
- Can AI act as a good game master without over-talking?

### Generated Interfaces

Questions:

- When should Alpha generate a UI versus stay conversational?
- What surfaces are needed for group experiences?
- How temporary should generated tools be?
- What does a great "mission card" feel like?

### Memory And Trust

Questions:

- What should Alpha remember?
- What should it forget?
- How can groups inspect, edit, or delete memory?
- How does consent work for shared memories?

### Economics

Questions:

- Would users pay for group experiences?
- Would creators sell experience templates?
- Could local businesses fulfill experience intents?
- When does Alpha become a demand marketplace?

### Safety And Boundaries

Questions:

- How does Alpha avoid manipulation?
- How does it avoid social pressure?
- How does it protect vulnerable users?
- How does it handle conflict inside groups?

## Research Methods

- Interview people about the last time a group chat became alive.
- Ask users to describe a desired social outcome, then manually create an Alpha-style experience.
- Run concierge pilots for 5 to 10 groups.
- Observe which missions produce action versus polite reactions.
- Compare personal, group, and creator-community onboarding.
- Test language: "quest," "mission," "ritual," "season," "world," "experience."

## Early Proof Signals

The MVP is working if:

- users invite other people without being asked
- groups complete a mission in the first week
- users save or share recaps
- users describe the experience using emotional language
- people ask Alpha to create the next thing
- the group does something offline
